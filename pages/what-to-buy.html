Negros Oriental · Signature Delicacy

Silvanas

Silvanas are the undisputed signature pasalubong of Dumaguete — a frozen dessert-cookie hybrid that has been delighting visitors since it was first perfected at Sans Rival Cakes and Pastries on Perdices Street. The name is said to derive from the Spanish word for "forest" or "wild," though the connection to nature is entirely in the imagination of whoever is eating one.

What makes Silvanas distinctive is the combination of textures: two crisp cashew meringue wafers sandwich a generous layer of French-style buttercream, and the entire confection is rolled in fine cookie crumbs until evenly coated. They are best eaten straight from the freezer — the buttercream firms to a consistency somewhere between ice cream and frosting, and the wafers stay perfectly crisp.

OriginDumaguete City, Negros Oriental
Best Bought AtSans Rival Café, Perdices St.

Negros Occidental · Iconic Dish

Chicken Inasal

Chicken Inasal is Bacolod's most celebrated contribution to Philippine cuisine — and arguably the most imitated dish in the country that has never been fully replicated outside its place of origin. The dish is deceptively simple: chicken marinated in a blend of calamansi juice, vinegar, lemongrass, ginger, and annatto oil, then grilled slowly over charcoal.

What makes the Bacolod version irreplaceable is the combination of the specific marinade ratios developed over decades at Manokan Country, the charcoal grill technique, and the serving ritual: garlic rice drizzled with chicken oil rendered from the annatto-infused marinade, alongside a small bowl of sinamak (spiced vinegar) and fresh tomatoes. The experience of eating it in an open-air stall at Manokan Country while the smoke rises around you is the authentic version — everything else is a reference.

Cultural Significance

  • Named one of the 10 greatest Filipino dishes by food critics
  • Spawned a national fast-food chain (Mang Inasal) with 500+ branches
  • Served paa (leg), pecho (breast), or batikulon (gizzard)
OriginBacolod City, Negros Occidental
Must Try AtManokan Country, Bacolod
Served WithGarlic rice, chicken oil, sinamak

Negros Occidental · Heritage Product

Muscovado Sugar

Muscovado is unrefined raw cane sugar from which the molasses has not been separated — the result is a dark, moist, deeply flavored sugar with a caramel-toffee richness that refined white sugar can never replicate. Negros Occidental produces the majority of the Philippines' sugar output, and muscovado is its most prized artisanal product: a direct expression of the island's centuries-old sugarcane heritage.

Unlike industrial sugar refining, muscovado production involves pressing the cane juice, boiling it in open kettles, and allowing it to partially dry without centrifugation — preserving the molasses naturally present in the cane. The resulting sugar is darker, more nutritious, and more complex in flavor. It is the essential ingredient in piaya, pastillas, and many traditional Negrense sweets, and has recently found an international market as a premium baking ingredient.

Uses
  • Key ingredient in piaya, pastillas, and Negrense kalamay
  • Used as a premium baking sweetener worldwide
  • Stirred into coffee and hot chocolate for depth
  • Marinade ingredient in adobo and BBQ glazes
OriginNegros Occidental province
Where to BuyBacolod public markets, pasalubong centers
Shelf LifeUp to 2 years if stored dry
Price Range₱80–₱150 per 500g pack
Negros Occidental · Pasalubong

Piaya

Piaya is a thin, round, flaky flatbread stuffed generously with muscovado sugar — baked on a hot griddle until the surface is golden and slightly charred, and the filling has caramelized into a chewy, molasses-rich center. It is the single most popular pasalubong from Bacolod, and the one product that most completely captures the identity of Negros Occidental in a single bite: sugarcane, craft, and sweetness.

The dough is made from flour, shortening, and water, kneaded and rolled to a near-paper thinness before being wrapped around a generous portion of muscovado. The finished piaya should be slightly crisp on the outside, yielding and sticky within, with a bittersweet molasses depth from the unrefined sugar. Variations include ube (purple yam), pandan, and cheese fillings, though purists insist the original muscovado version is the only one that matters.

Negros Occidental · Heritage Drink

Tablea de Cacao

Tablea are pure, unsweetened cacao tablets made from locally grown and fermented cacao beans. They are the base ingredient of sikwate — the traditional Filipino hot chocolate that has been consumed on these islands since long before Spanish colonization introduced the concept of sweetened cocoa to European kitchens.

Making tablea is labor-intensive: ripe cacao pods are harvested, the beans are fermented in banana leaves for several days to develop their flavor, then sun-dried, roasted, and ground by hand or stone mill into a thick paste. The paste is then formed into small discs or balls and dried again. The resulting tablets are intensely aromatic, slightly bitter, and carry the unmistakable terroir of Negros soil. When dissolved in hot water with a pinch of salt and optionally sweetened with muscovado, the result is something completely unlike commercial hot chocolate.

How to Use
  • Dissolve 2–3 tablets in hot water, whisk until frothy (sikwate)
  • Add muscovado sugar to taste — never white sugar for authenticity
  • Pair with puto maya (sticky rice cake) for the traditional Visayan breakfast
  • Used in baking brownies, cakes, and champorado (chocolate rice porridge)
OriginNegros Occidental cacao farms
Ingredients100% pure cacao — no additives
Where to BuyBacolod pasalubong shops, markets
Price Range₱60–₱120 per pack
Negros Oriental · Traditional Kakanin

Baye-Baye

Baye-Baye is a traditional kakanin (rice cake) native to Negros Oriental, made from young coconut (buko) meat, glutinous rice flour, and coconut milk — cooked together until thick and sticky, then shaped by hand into small cylinders or wrapped in coconut leaves for gifting. It is a deeply local delicacy that rarely travels beyond the province, making it one of the most authentic food souvenirs a visitor can bring home.

The texture is chewy and dense in a thoroughly satisfying way — the young coconut adding a subtle fresh sweetness and a slight fibrous quality that distinguishes it from other sticky rice cakes. It is typically sold at local markets and roadside stalls rather than tourist shops, which makes finding it part of the experience of exploring Negros Oriental off the beaten path.

Negros Occidental · Festival Craft

MassKara Festival Mask

The MassKara mask is the most iconic symbol of Bacolod City — a perpetually smiling face adorned with bright flowers, sequins, mirrors, and colorful embellishments that represent the Negrense spirit of resilience and joy. Born from the 1980 MassKara Festival as a symbol of defiance against hardship, the mask has since become a cultural artifact recognized far beyond the Philippines.

Festival-grade masks are wearable works of art: hand-painted, hand-decorated, and often incorporating real dried or silk flowers, rhinestones, and intricate filigree patterns unique to each maker. Souvenir versions range from simple painted plaster to elaborately decorated collector pieces. The best masks are made by artisan families in Barangay Mandalagan, Bacolod, where mask-making has been a livelihood for generations.

Features & Unique Qualities
  • Each mask is hand-painted and entirely unique — no two are identical
  • Decorated with dried/silk flowers, sequins, mirrors, and rhinestones
  • Festival-grade masks are judged competitively during MassKara
  • Available as wall art, wearable masks, or miniature souvenirs
OriginBacolod City, Negros Occidental
Where to BuyBacolod public market, SM Bacolod
MaterialPapier-mâché, plaster, or fiber
Price Range₱150 (souvenir) to ₱2,000+ (artisan)
Negros Island · Traditional Craft

Rattan & Bamboo Crafts

Rattan and bamboo weaving is one of the oldest craft traditions in Negros Island — practiced by indigenous and lowland communities across both provinces for centuries. The abundant rattan forests and bamboo groves of the Negros highlands have long supplied raw materials for functional objects: baskets, trays, storage containers, furniture frames, and decorative pieces that combine utility with artisanal beauty.

Contemporary Negrense rattan craft has evolved considerably — local artisans now produce export-quality furniture, home décor, and fashion accessories that compete in international markets. The town of Isabela in Negros Occidental is particularly known for its weaving cooperatives, where multiple generations of families continue refining techniques passed down through oral tradition. Buying these items directly from cooperatives or local markets supports these communities directly.

What to Look For
  • Palayok baskets (traditional round baskets with lids)
  • Woven table mats and placemats — lightweight for travel
  • Rattan handbags and fashion accessories
  • Bamboo furniture and decorative items for home use
OriginAcross Negros Island
Where to BuyLocal markets, cooperative shops
Best ForHome décor, unique gifts, fashion
Price Range₱100 (small items) to ₱3,000+ (furniture)
Negros Oriental · Signature Dessert

Sans Rival Cake

Sans Rival — French for "without rival" — is a layered Filipino dessert cake of Philippine-French heritage that found its most celebrated expression in Dumaguete. The cake consists of multiple layers of crisp cashew-studded dacquoise meringue (a French-style nut meringue) sandwiched with rich French-style buttercream, assembled and refrigerated until the layers meld into a single dense, intensely flavored confection.

The origins of Sans Rival in the Philippines are traced to Filipino chefs who trained in Paris in the early 20th century and brought French pâtisserie techniques home. The Dumaguete version — most famously made by Sans Rival Cakes and Pastries, established on Perdices Street — is considered the gold standard: its cashew layer is thicker, its buttercream richer, and its assembly tighter than any imitation.

Negros Island · Artisanal Condiment

Native Cane Vinegar

Sugarcane vinegar — known locally as sukang tubó — is one of the most important condiments in Negrense cooking, and arguably the most underappreciated artisanal product the island produces. Made by fermenting fresh sugarcane juice through a two-stage fermentation process (first alcoholic, then acetic), the result is a mild, slightly sweet, straw-colored vinegar with a complexity that commercial white vinegar cannot approach.

The most famous version is sinamak — sukang tubó infused with bird's eye chili, garlic, ginger, and black pepper — which is served alongside every plate of Chicken Inasal in Bacolod and used as a dipping sauce, marinade, and condiment throughout both provinces. Artisanal producers still ferment their vinegar in clay jars or wooden barrels, aging it for months before bottling.

Key Uses
  • Essential dipping sauce alongside Chicken Inasal (as sinamak)
  • Base for kinilaw (Filipino ceviche) — especially with fresh tuna
  • Marinade for pork and chicken dishes
  • Salad dressing with garlic and native pepper
OriginNegros Island sugarcane farms
Where to BuyPublic markets, pasalubong centers
Best VersionSinamak (spiced) — ask for homemade
Price Range₱40–₱100 per bottle
